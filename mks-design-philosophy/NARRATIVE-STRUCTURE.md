# The Narrative of Michael Kim Sheng

## How to Use This Document

This is the true story, structured as narrative architecture. It is organized in acts — not chronological biography, but the emotional arc that the music, the brand, and the website all follow. Each act contains:

- The real events and truths (from Mike's own words)
- Key quotes (verbatim, italicized)
- The design implications (how this act translates to visual/UX decisions)
- The taste principle (what this act teaches about the artistic philosophy)

Pull from this document whenever you need to write copy, make a visual decision, or justify a design direction. The story is the source code. Everything else compiles from it.

# PROLOGUE: THE KNOWING

## The Truth

Michael Kim Sheng did not discover music. Music was already in him. There was no origin story in the traditional sense — no moment of first hearing a piano and being changed. The knowing preceded the doing. The identity of "composer" existed before the ability to compose.

This matters because it reframes everything that follows. The training, the struggle, the tension with survival — none of it is the story of someone trying to become something. It's the story of someone who already was something, navigating a world that kept asking him to be something else.

## Key Quotes

> *"I feel like I've always known I was going to want to write music. I felt like I was always going to be a composer no matter what."*

> *"No matter what happens to you, you will always follow your passion and no matter what direction you go you'll always be making music."*

> *"No matter how far I stray from it, I'll always return to music."*

## The Structural Pattern

**The return.** This is the foundational pattern of everything — the music, the life, the audience's experience. Departure and return. Straying and coming back. The prodigal pattern. It is not a straight line forward. It is a circle that expands each time it completes.

## Design Implications

- The site should feel like a return — like you've been here before even on first visit
- Circular/recurring motifs over linear progression
- The landing page is not an introduction. It's a homecoming.

## Taste Principle

**Music is not a career choice. It is an orientation.** The way some people are oriented toward language or toward numbers, Mike is oriented toward sound. This distinction matters: the website does not present an artist who chose music. It presents someone the music chose.

# ACT I: THE SKETCHING PHASE

## The Truth

The handwritten scores are not finished products. They are the exploration itself — the act of searching for the moment of excitement. Mike sits with blank staff paper and jots down ideas, moving through them until something catches. He calls this the "sketching phase." The score is the search, not the answer. The answer is the nugget of joy that arrives mid-search.

The piece "Lake" was the turning point. A client needed film music. Mike assumed simplicity would be easy. Instead, simplicity became the revelation. The complicated music he'd been trained to admire was not where his voice lived. His voice lived in the simple thing done with devastating emotional precision.

## Key Quotes

> *"A handwritten score is an exploration phase where you're trying to find that element of music that excites you. When you feel that nugget of joy, that's when you kind of begin to really make music. It's your sketching phase."*

> *"Film can't be so complicated. And I realized simple music is something I'd want to write more of."*

> *"We start off with a brush stroke because you feel something. You see a watercolor maybe of a tree in a foggy landscape. You now start affecting a soft—"*

## The Structural Pattern

**The search that becomes the thing.** The process is the product. The sketching phase is not preliminary — it IS the work. The unfinished quality of exploration is what gives the music its emotional openness. A perfectly resolved composition would be closed. A sketch stays open. The listener enters through the openings.

## Design Implications

- The score sheets on the site should feel unfinished, in-process — not mounted and framed
- The cursor-reveal interaction is the user joining the sketching phase. They are searching with Mike.
- The site itself should feel like an exploration — not a finished brochure but an environment you move through and discover

## Taste Principle

**Create what excites you, not what impresses.** The shift from complex-to-prove-skill to simple-to-feel was the artistic breakthrough. The website must embody this: no complexity for its own sake. Every element justified by whether it creates excitement or feeling, not by whether it demonstrates technical ability.

> *"You actually need to create the best work over your technical skill set in some ways."*

# ACT II: THE VOICE

## The Truth

There is a specific moment in an artist's development where music stops being a demonstration of skill and starts being a language. Mike has crossed this line. Music is no longer something he performs — it is how he speaks. The medium has become transparent. He doesn't think about technique; he thinks about what he wants to say, and the technique serves it.

This is why taste matters more than training. Taste — real taste, not taste as social performance — comes from knowing who you are and refusing to let that be shaped by the desire for approval. The voice emerges when you stop writing for acceptance and start writing from identity.

## Key Quotes

> *"You get to a point where music doesn't become something about your technique or your ability to show off, but it becomes like you start to speak like that. Music is an extension of you. You're using the medium to speak a language."*

> *"It's not like I'm going to show off this technique that I can do. Let me tell you how I feel through a medium that I understand very well."*

> *"Taste is not tainted by what others' opinions of you are. Don't let your taste be skewed by wanting to be accepted. Taste is coming from something within where it represents who you are and how you feel. When you find that, you kind of walk into your own voice as an artist."*

## The Structural Pattern

**The medium becomes invisible.** When someone is truly fluent in a language, you don't hear the grammar — you hear the meaning. Mike's music has reached this stage. The orchestration, the harmony, the notation — these are grammar. The listener never hears them. They hear: longing, return, the door opening to a room they forgot existed.

## Design Implications

- The website's design should be invisible. The user should not think "this is a well-designed website." They should think "I feel something."
- Technical craft (animations, interactions, code) serves the feeling. If you notice the CSS, the CSS has failed.
- The portfolio of work should never foreground technique. No "composed for 60-piece orchestra" as a selling point. Instead: what the piece makes you feel.

## Taste Principle

**Your voice is what remains when you stop performing.** The website's "voice" is what it feels like when every decoration is stripped away. If the site still has emotional impact in a wireframe, the voice is real. If it only works with the polish, the polish is hiding an absence.

> *"A lot of people hear that they found their sound, they found their way, their marketing, their vibe. Music is an extension of you."*

# ACT III: THE TENSION

## The Truth

The threat to the music was never creative. It was economic. Survival and art occupy the same hours in a day, and survival wins by default because it has to. When Mike's family "tried to sweep the rug from under his feet," the immediate response was not to create — it was fight or flight. Art is a luxury when you're drowning.

But the tension produced its own kind of clarity. When writing for survival (clients, commercial work), the music starts serving someone else's conception of how it will be received. It becomes genuine to a market instead of genuine to the self. Both are music. But one feels authentic and the other feels like work.

The tension never fully resolves. It is ongoing. The fear of survival pulls music away. The need to create pulls it back. This oscillation IS the rhythm of the artistic life.

## Key Quotes

> *"Whenever I have discussions about survival and making money, music gets pulled away from me."*

> *"When my family tried to sweep the rug from under my feet. It was pretty stressful."*

> *"Art can come out from anything, from your most stressful moments as well."*

> *"When it comes to a moment where you feel where you're fearing for survival, creating art is not your first response. You're going to think fight or flight. Art is sort of a luxury in some ways."*

> *"You start to make music that doesn't really resonate with your soul but resonates with your conception of how it will be received. And that is probably still music. But it's not genuine to you; it's genuine to someone else."*

> *"Music doesn't have any direct monetary value, but the value is based on the effect it has on society and how it makes people feel."*

## The Structural Pattern

**The sustained note without a root.** Mike described the tension musically: strings held high, hovering, sustained for too long. No bass note. No foundation. Everything floating without resolution. The tension is not dramatic — it's the absence of ground. Then: the bass note arrives. Not because you went looking for it. It hits when it hits.

> *"I hear sustained lines that are just being sustained for too long while things seem kind of hovering. It's missing some sort of base note, and then the resolution is when the base note comes in."*

> *"It hits when it hits. You'll just know that's the base note when it's the base note."*

## The Piece Born From This Conversation

During this discussion, Mike spontaneously composed a piece in his head:

> *"I'm gonna have strings really high up. And it's gonna be like a minute of just dang dang dang, like moving. And there's like a piano playing some chords. Everything's in a high register. And then like two minutes in, just gonna be like a brass, and all the strings will come in at the same time, and the piano will come in, and it's just gonna play these long chords, and the violins are gonna keep going fast. And it's just gonna go for another two minutes and move on."*

This is proof of concept: the story generates the music in real time. The life and the work are not separate.

## Design Implications

- The "hovering" feeling — elements that float without clear grounding — should exist in the landing page. The bass note is the scroll beginning. The resolution is the content arriving.
- The tension between art and survival maps to the tension between atmospheric design and functional commerce. The site is art. The store is survival. Both must coexist without one killing the other.
- The unresolved oscillation is a feature, not a bug. The site should have moments of unresolved tension — sections that don't fully conclude before the next one begins.

## Taste Principle

**The value of music is not monetary. It is the effect on the listener.** The website's "value proposition" is not "buy this album." It is: "this music will make you feel something you forgot you could feel." The transaction follows the experience, never the reverse.

# ACT IV: THE BOREDOM THAT CREATED TASTE

## The Truth

Mike's sound did not come from inspiration. It came from boredom. Years of classical training meant years of mandatory listening to repertoire that, however historically significant, felt interminable. 45-minute pieces. Pretentious structures. Music that valued tradition over feeling.

The boredom produced a counter-instinct: make music that is the appropriate length. Four minutes. Five minutes. Music that changes enough to sustain interest, repeats enough to create familiarity, and has genuine powerful moments — without going on forever. The listener's time is respected because Mike knows what it feels like to have his time disrespected.

He is a self-described "proud pleaser" — not in the pejorative sense of seeking approval, but in the constructive sense of wanting the audience to have an experience worth their attention. He writes music he himself wants to hear, and trusts that what moves him will move others.

## Key Quotes

> *"I've been so bored by hearing so much shit music from my childhood. You're forced to listen to music that was culturally significant 200 years ago. At a certain point you're like, why am I always having to listen to this same song and it's like 45 minutes long."*

> *"I want to make something that's appropriate length. Four minutes, five minutes. That you can sit through and enjoy the moments because it's not going on forever."*

> *"I'm a proud pleaser in that way where I want to create something that changes a lot but is repetitive enough and has its powerful moments and has its depth."*

> *"I try to give people art that I want to hear."*

> *"That doesn't mean I love everything I make but I aim to make what I love."*

## The Structural Pattern

**Density over duration.** The classical tradition stretched time. Mike compresses it. A four-minute MKS piece contains the emotional arc that a Romantic-era composer would have spread across a movement. The density IS the innovation — the same emotional journey, delivered with modern respect for attention.

## Design Implications

- The website should be dense, not long. Fewer sections with more emotional impact per section, rather than an endless scroll.
- Respect the user's time the way Mike respects the listener's time. No filler sections. No "padding." Every viewport earns its existence.
- The interactions should change enough to sustain interest (rotating nature elements, temperature shifts) but repeat enough to create familiarity (the score-line motif, the teal/amber system, the grain overlay).

## Taste Principle

**Boredom is the teacher.** Knowing what you don't want to make is as important as knowing what you do. The classical training was necessary — not for the techniques it taught, but for the taste it sharpened by negative example. The website should feel like the opposite of a default SquareSpace template the same way the music feels like the opposite of a 45-minute symphonic movement.

# ACT V: THE AUDIENCE

## The Truth

The people who listen to Michael Kim Sheng's music are not casual listeners. They are music appreciators — people with eclectic taste who actively explore, who use music intentionally rather than as background. They tend to be either older (the Facebook demographic — 60+) or people of any age who have found refuge in music. People for whom music genuinely makes life better.

They are escaping the "perpetual grey of reality" and simultaneously pursuing "the feelings that make life so colorful." Both at once. The escape and the pursuit are the same act.

What they report experiencing:

- Peace and tranquility
- Memories of past loved ones
- Memories of home
- The feeling of being transported — the SpongeBob meme of being airlifted with earbuds in
- The Squidward meme of crying with the volume past max
- Being taken to a different place

One commenter wrote: *"This felt like catching a glimpse of a long lost loved one in the crowd. When you go to follow them they're already gone. To wonder if you imagined it at all, yet hopeful."*

## Key Quotes

> *"Music appreciators beyond just putting music in the background. People who have more eclectic tastes and like to explore music."*

> *"Either older people generally or people who have found refuge in music. People who genuinely feel that music makes their lives better."*

> *"It's an escape from the perpetual grey of reality but then also in pursuit of the feelings that make life so colorful."*

> *"People are able to connect to what I'm writing because of that. I feel like people love what part of my imagination I'm connecting to."*

> *"The emotions that communicate through music resonate with them, essentially."*

## The Two Audiences

**The existing audience (Facebook, 60+):** They believe the sunsets are real. They would believe AI-generated content. They respond with sincerity. They share with friends. They comment with emotional testimony. They are the proof that the music works.

**The target audience (younger, discovery-oriented):** They haven't found MKS yet. They are the ones who would send the website link to a friend. They need the "woah" factor — the moment of encountering something that feels different from everything else on the internet. The website is built primarily for them, because the music already serves the existing audience. The website's job is acquisition.

## Design Implications

- The site speaks to the younger discovery audience in design language (medium-heavy interaction, cinematic pacing, "woah" moments) while honoring the existing audience's emotional relationship (the music is accessible, the store is clear, nothing is confusing)
- The community indicators (listening-now counters, comment highlights) should feature real audience testimony — not as testimonials but as proof of resonance
- The top comment from the audience ("catching a glimpse of a long lost loved one in the crowd") is better copy than anything a designer could write. Use real words from real listeners.

## Taste Principle

**The audience completes the circuit.** Mike writes from his imagination. The listener connects to that imagination through their own memories and losses. The music is the bridge — it doesn't create the feeling, it finds the feeling that was already in the listener. The website should do the same: not inject emotion, but create the conditions for the visitor's own emotion to surface.

# ACT VI: THE PERSONA

## The Truth

The scroll photo — the figure carrying a music scroll with a white horse — is not documentation. It is persona construction. Mike is deliberately creating a visual separation between himself and ordinary life. This is not elitism. It is the necessary act of establishing that an artist occupies a different role than a civilian.

The image says: this is not someone you see on the street. This is someone who carries a scroll of handwritten music with a horse following them through a landscape of light. The image creates aura. The aura creates the desire to engage. The desire creates the audience.

## Key Quotes

> *"This photo is supposed to make me look like a star. Like an emerging artist that is not easily accessible."*

> *"It's supposed to separate me from Joe at the grocery store. It's creating an artist representation. Persona."*

> *"This is not someone you see on the street. This is an artist."*

## The Structural Pattern

**Separation as invitation.** By establishing distance (the persona is elevated, not casual), Mike creates the desire to close that distance. You can't long for something that's already accessible. The inaccessibility IS the marketing. This is the Coca-Cola principle applied to identity: you see the condensation, you want the drink. You see the figure with the scroll, you want to know the music.

## Design Implications

- Mike's presence on the site should feel curated, not casual. No "hey guys" energy. The About section is not a blog post — it's a portrait.
- The artist image on the site should maintain the scroll-photo energy: cinematic, separated, elevated. Shot from the audience's perspective (looking up/toward, not selfie-angle).
- The persona is the bridge between the cold design of the site and the warm reality of the music. The site is the landscape. Mike is the figure in it.

## Taste Principle

**Aura is manufactured, but the thing inside the aura must be real.** The persona is deliberate. The scroll photo is staged. The separation from "Joe at the grocery store" is a conscious choice. This is not fake — it is presentation. The difference: the music behind the persona is genuine. The persona gets you to the door. The music is what's behind it.

# THE MUSICAL ARCHITECTURE

## How the Story Sounds

Based on Mike's spontaneous description of the piece that emerged from this conversation:

### Movement I — The Hovering (0:00 - 1:00)
Strings high up. Moving fast. A piano playing chords. Everything in a high register. No bass. No foundation. The sustained note without a root. This is the tension, the survival fear, the ground swept away. Beautiful but unresolved. You're floating and you don't know where the floor is.

**Website parallel:** The landing page. Dark. Hovering elements. No navigation visible. The user doesn't know where they are yet.

### Movement II — The Bass Note Arrives (1:00 - 2:00)
Brass enters. All the strings come in simultaneously. The piano comes in. Long chords. The violins keep going fast on top. The root has been found. The resolution hits not because you searched for it, but because it was time.

**Website parallel:** The scroll begins. Content arrives. The name resolves. The nature element appears. The cold world gets its first warm moment.

### Movement III — The Continuation (2:00 - 4:00)
It just goes. The energy sustains. It moves on.

**Website parallel:** The body of the site. Music section, About, Store, Tour. The experience continues. The resolution doesn't end — it evolves.

# EPILOGUE: THE SENTENCE, REVISED

The original sentence:

> Michael Kim Sheng makes music that finds the locked rooms inside you, opens them, and shows you that what you thought you lost was still there — and the discovery of that is so beautiful it makes you cry.

The revised sentence, informed by the full story:

**Michael Kim Sheng has always been a composer. The music was never a choice — it was an orientation. Through classical training that taught him what he didn't want to be, through survival that tried to pull the pen from his hand, through the boredom of tradition and the terror of losing ground, the music persisted. It came out simple when everyone expected complicated. It came out honest when the market wanted performance. It landed in four minutes what the old world took forty-five to say. And when people hear it, they don't hear technique. They hear the door to a room they forgot they had — and it's already open, and the light is coming in, and they're crying because the thing they lost was never gone. It was just waiting for someone to write the note that unlocked it.**
