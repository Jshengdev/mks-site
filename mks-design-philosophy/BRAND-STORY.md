# The Story of Michael Kim Sheng

## The Scaffolding

This is not a bio. This is the narrative architecture beneath everything — the site, the music, the visual identity. Every design decision traces back to this story.

## The Image He Carries

A figure walks forward. He carries a music scroll — the handwritten scores, the body of work, the craft itself held in his hands. Not hidden, not protected in a case. Carried openly, the way you carry something you've committed your life to. Behind him: a white horse. Not ridden. Following. Above: a sky breaking open. Light pours through a cloud mass in visible rays. The sun is hidden — you can't see the source. But the light reaches everything. It touches the ground. It touches the figure. It touches you looking at it.

## Why the Music Sounds the Way It Does

### The Leash and the Liberation

There was a leash. There's always a leash — the thing that held, constrained, defined the boundaries of the possible. Family expectation, cultural weight, the gap between the person you were raised to be and the person the music was making you become. The leash is not the villain. It was real. It had reasons. But the music was the thing that couldn't be leashed.

The scores are handwritten because the music came through the hand before it went anywhere else. Not typed into software. Not clicked into a grid. Written — the way you write a letter to someone you need to reach. The pen touching paper is the first moment the internal becomes external. That's why the scores matter. They're proof of the crossing.

### The Sound of Forgotten Rooms

People cry. They say: "I remembered something I forgot." The music reaches into rooms people sealed shut — childhood kitchens, the way someone's hand felt, the specific light of an afternoon that ended twenty years ago. The music doesn't create these memories. It finds the door that was locked and opens it.

This is why the music sounds orchestral but intimate. Why it sounds vast but personal. It has the scale of a landscape (the sky, the ocean, the plains) but the specificity of a single human memory (the way your mother hummed, the creak of a specific floorboard). The vastness is not for grandeur. It's because the feelings are that big. They need that much room.

### The Grief That Isn't Sad

> "It should help you feel like you're longing for something. Like you've lost something. Like you're grieving."

But the tears are happy tears. This is the paradox at the center of everything: the music makes you feel loss, and the feeling of loss is beautiful, and the beauty of it is what makes you cry, and the crying is relief. You didn't know you were carrying that until the music put it down for you.

The grief is not for something dead. It's for something alive that you forgot was alive. The music reminds you. The reminder hurts because it's so good.

## The Life That Made This

### The Plains

Open fields. Vast sky. The feeling of being small inside something enormous and being okay with it. This is the geography of the music — not a city, not a room, but the open land where you can see the horizon in every direction. The plains teach you that you are not the biggest thing. The music carries this lesson: you are small, the feeling is big, and the bigness is the gift.

### The Crossing of Worlds

East and West. Classical training and something that can't be trained. The discipline of notation — every note placed, every dynamic marked, every rest measured — and the wildness of what the notation is trying to capture. The scores are the bridge: Western music tradition as the vessel for something that doesn't fully belong to any single tradition. The music is culturally hybrid in the way that only a person who has lived between worlds can make.

### The Hand

Everything passes through the hand. Handwritten scores. Hands on keys. The physicality of making music in a world that has digitized everything. This is not nostalgia — it's insistence. The hand is where intention becomes real. The hand is the proof that a human being made this, not a process.

### The Refusal of the Fake

> "I think part of the magic is doing it like non-AI. I think people would respect that."

The sunsets are real. The pointing videos are real. The locations are real. In a world drowning in generated content, the realness is the flex. Not because AI is bad — but because authenticity is scarce, and scarcity creates value, and value creates the desire Mike wants the website to produce.

## The Four Seasons of MKS

The music moves through emotional seasons. The website mirrors this.

### Winter (The Landing)
Cold. Vast. Dark sky with light breaking through. The moment before the music starts. You don't know what's coming but your body is already preparing. The score sheets fly through the sky — the music liberated from the page, unbound, free. The leash is gone.

### Spring (The Music / Listen)
The ocean. Things beneath the surface rising up. Cursor parts the water and the scores are revealed below. The music plays and the frozen world begins to thaw. Colors warm slightly. Motion increases. Life is returning.

### Summer (The Store)
Golden hour. The warmest room. Products bathed in their own worlds — a CD on a grassy field with wind moving through it, another in amber light. This is the peak of warmth. Desire is at its highest. The Coca-Cola moment. You want to hold the thing.

### Autumn (The Departure)
The dusk sky. The fade. The sunset has already happened. What remains is the afterglow and the specific beautiful sadness of something ending. The footer dissolves. The user leaves carrying the feeling.
